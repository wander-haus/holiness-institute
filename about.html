Our Purpose

The Holiness Institute exists to further the work of Saint John Paul II in aiding the Church to come to renewal in Jesus Christ, as he enunciated it in Novo Millennio Ineunte and Pastores Gregis, with his teaching patrimony as the sure guide.

At the threshold of the new millennium, John Paul did not give the Church a new program. He insisted that “the programme already exists”: Christ Himself, “to be known, loved and imitated, so that in him we may live the life of the Trinity” (Novo Millennio Ineunte, 29). And he gave the Church her rule of judgment: “all pastoral initiatives must be set in relation to holiness” (Novo Millennio Ineunte, 30).

The Institute takes that rule seriously, and takes it as given. We do not offer another strategy, another system, or another set of techniques. We work so that holiness — union with God in love — may again govern the life of the Church in practice and not only in vocabulary: in the soul, in the family, in the parish, in the priesthood, and in the episcopate.

Our Theological Principles

Five principles govern everything the Institute does. They are not original to us. They are the Church’s own, retrieved and placed back in their proper order.

I.Christocentricity

Christ is “the center of the universe and of history” (Redemptor Hominis, 1). The Church has no life apart from Him and no fruitfulness except by abiding in Him. Renewal therefore never begins with what the Church must do. It begins with who God is, what He is already giving, and the Son in whom He has given everything.

II.Sacramental Life

The Church is “in Christ like a sacrament” (Lumen Gentium, 1), and the Christian life begins in baptism: real consecration, ordered toward sanctification, ordered toward holiness. The sacraments are not ceremonies attached to a managed institution. They are the ordinary places where Christ acts, and everything else in the Church exists to dispose souls to receive what He gives there.

III.The Primacy of Grace

God acts first. It is fatal — John Paul’s word — to think that results depend on our ability to act and to plan (cf. Novo Millennio Ineunte, 38). The primacy of grace includes abandonment: the soul’s trustful surrender to Divine Providence, which is not passivity but the deepest cooperation, because it ceases to substitute its own control for God’s action.

IV.The Necessity of Prayer

Prayer is not one activity among others. It is the ordinary path by which belief becomes trust, trust becomes abandonment, and abandonment becomes self-gift. A Church that bypasses prayer, or uses it as decoration rather than governance, cannot be renewed, because prayer is where the Church receives the One she is called to give.

V.Witness to Love

Holiness is the perfection of charity, and charity is fecund. The holy soul becomes gift: bread for others, mercy for sinners, witness for the Church. The world will not be persuaded by a more efficient Church. It will be drawn by a Church radiant with the life of Christ — by saints, by families that are seminaries of love, by fathers who give life. Witness follows union. Mission follows holiness.
