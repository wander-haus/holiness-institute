The 8S describe the visible articulation of diocesan life. The Four Causes determine whether that articulation is ordered toward sanctification or toward self-preservation.

The framework is not imported corporate logic but an analytic instrument placed beneath the Church's own metaphysical commitments. The theology governs the framework. The framework does not govern the theology. Used rightly, it functions as a mirror rather than a mechanism.

## The 8S Re-Ordered by Causality

### 1. Superordinate Goal — Final Cause

**Holiness in Jesus Christ.** Union with Christ is the final cause; conformity to Christ (*ipsus Christus*) is the formal expression.

*Diagnostic: Is holiness the real governing end?*

### 2. Strategies — Secondary Efficient Cause

Strategies dispose subjects to receive grace. They do not generate sanctity.

*Diagnostic: Do strategies protect prayer or replace it?*

### 3. Structure — Secondary Material Cause

Institutional arrangements that serve sacramental ecology.

*Diagnostic: Does structure facilitate sanctification?*

### 4. Systems — Instrumental Efficient Cause

Procedural tools that support but cannot produce holiness.

*Diagnostic: Are systems trusted more than prayer?*

### 5. Staffing — Distributive Ordering

Placement aligned with sacramental ontology. Priests first assigned to what only priests can do.

*Diagnostic: Is sacramental capacity protected?*

### 6. Skills — Primary Material Cause

Ontology perfected by habit and virtue. Operative habits joined with infused virtue.

*Diagnostic: Are we forming saints or professionals?*

### 7. Style — Formal Expressive

Paternal versus managerial. Style reveals interior form.

*Diagnostic: Charity or urgency?*

### 8. Shared Values — Formal Cause

What is truly loved and protected. Tradition received versus culture negotiated.

*Diagnostic: What does our culture actually reward?*

## The Four Causes

| Cause | Primary Ultimate | Proximate |
|-------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Final** | Union with the Trinity | Holiness |
| **Formal** | Jesus Christ as exemplar | Conformity to Christ |
| **Efficient** | The Trinity | Sacraments (instrumental), priests and parents (secondary) |
| **Material** | Human person | Soul, parish, family, formation |

The decisive development lies not in the eightfold articulation itself but in subordinating the entire structure to theological and metaphysical principles. Within ecclesial life, the Superordinate Goal cannot remain undefined. The Second Vatican Council declares that "all in the Church... are called to holiness" and that this holiness consists in "the perfection of charity" (*Lumen Gentium*, 39–40).

When so ordered, the 8S serve the Church's renewal not by producing fruit, but by helping her recognize whether she remains attached to the Vine from whom alone fruit can come.
