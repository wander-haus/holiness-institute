Christ Speaks First: "What Does It Profit a Man?"

"What does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his
soul?"
(Mark 8:36)

With this question Christ exposes a perennial confusion between apparent
success and true fruitfulness. What appears as gain according to one
logic may coincide with loss according to another. Activity may increase
while sanctity declines. Visibility may expand while communion weakens.
The distinction is not moralistic but metaphysical. It concerns ends and
causes. It concerns what ultimately governs action and what therefore
defines fruit.

In contemporary pastoral life two distinct logics frequently operate
side by side. The first is strategic. It begins with humanly determined
objectives, identifies measurable outcomes, and organizes resources in
order to achieve them. Its governing questions are pragmatic: What
works? What scales? What sustains? What produces visible results? The
second logic is theological. It begins not with projected outcomes but
with a received end, union with God in Christ. Its first questions are
different: Does this deepen charity? Does this protect prayer? Does this
conform wills to the Father? Both logics may use similar language. Both
may speak of mission. Both may cite Scripture. Yet their interior
grammar differs. One seeks to produce outcomes. The other seeks to
cooperate with grace. One measures success by expansion and efficiency.
The other measures fruit by sanctification. One accelerates under
pressure. The other slows under discernment. The difference is not
cosmetic. It is causal.

The Church herself provides the governing horizon. The Second Vatican
Council teaches that "the Church, in Christ, is in the nature of a
sacrament, that is, a sign and instrument of intimate union with God and
of the unity of all mankind" (Lumen Gentium, 1). If the Church is
sacrament before she is system, communion before she is structure, then
her operative logic must correspond to that identity. Saint John Paul II
makes the consequence explicit when he writes, "I have no hesitation in
saying that all pastoral initiatives must be set in relation to
holiness" (Novo Millennio Ineunte, 30). He immediately adds that this is
"a choice filled with consequences" (NMI, 31). The distinction between
these two logics therefore is not managerial preference but theological
obedience.

This difference is not merely methodological. It is metaphysical.
Holiness Planning differs from strategic planning not in intensity but
in kind. When the supernatural logic governs, strategy is not eliminated
but subordinated. Structures remain, but they no longer define success.
Coordination continues, yet it no longer claims generative power.

Strategic planning in itself is not hostile to the Church. Within the
natural order it serves legitimate purposes. Prudence requires
coordination. Resources must be stewarded. Institutions must function.
The distortion occurs when strategic logic becomes primary. When this
happens pastoral life begins with analysis rather than abiding. Needs
assessments precede examination of conscience. Efficiency is prized and
speed becomes virtue. Religious language is gradually reinterpreted
functionally and "leaders" quietly replace stewards. Holiness is not
denied; it is assumed. Grace is not rejected; it is backgrounded. Prayer
remains, yet it is instrumentalized and demoted to a preparatory role
rather than acknowledged as primary efficient cause. Over time this
logic reshapes ecclesial imagination. The Church is treated as an
organization to be optimized rather than as a sacramental organism to be
sanctified. Problems are defined materially and addressed materially.
Exhaustion is interpreted as logistical overload rather than spiritual
signal. The logic remains coherent within its own terms, yet it no
longer corresponds to the Church's nature.

Holiness Planning proceeds from a different first principle. The Church
does not generate her mission; she receives it. Her final cause is
given, not invented. Her efficient cause is divine, not self-produced.
Human cooperation is real but secondary. This logic does not abolish
planning. It reorders it. Initiatives arise from prayer rather than
urgency. Structures are evaluated by their capacity to sanctify rather
than by their complexity. Speed yields to discernment. The governing
question becomes not first what can be done, but what should be done in
obedience. This approach may appear slower and it may produce fewer
visible initiatives. Yet it preserves interior freedom and protects
charity because it refuses to ask secondary causes to carry the weight
of primary ones.

The divergence between these logics becomes most visible in how they
understand growth. Strategic logic is mechanical. Inputs yield outputs.
More energy produces more results. Coordination secures efficiency.
Holiness logic is organic. Growth proceeds from within and often
invisibly. It requires time and cannot be forced without damage. Saint
Paul writes that Christ is the Head "from whom the whole body, joined
and knit together... makes bodily growth and builds itself up in love"
(Ephesians 4:16). Growth comes "from him." The body builds itself up "in
love." When the Church is treated mechanically, she is pressed to grow
by compression and acceleration. When treated organically, she grows by
deepening communion.

One of the most seductive features of strategic logic is that it
produces measurable reassurance. Numbers can be reported, charts
displayed, and momentum demonstrated. Holiness logic, by contrast, often
yields fruit that is quiet and difficult to quantify. Confessions
increase gradually. Vocations deepen invisibly before they appear
publicly. Families reconcile in hidden fidelity. Priests persevere
interiorly long before that perseverance bears visible fruit. Strategic
logic comforts by visibility. Holiness logic demands trust. This is why
the two logics coexist uneasily. Under pressure, the temptation is to
privilege what can be displayed over what can only be discerned.

At root, the divergence rests upon anthropology. Strategic logic treats
persons primarily as capacities to be mobilized. Time is allocated,
tasks are assigned, performance is evaluated, and output is measured.
Holiness logic treats persons as souls to be sanctified. Time is guarded
for prayer. Burdens are measured by interior freedom. Fidelity outweighs
output. The first approach risks exhaustion because it implicitly makes
the human agent principal cause. The second requires patience because it
acknowledges that "without Christ you can do nothing" (John 15:5).
Neither logic excludes prudence. Only one corresponds to the Church's
supernatural end.

In an era of institutional pressure and cultural decline, the appeal of
strategic logic intensifies. Urgency invites acceleration. Anxiety
invites control. Leaders feel compelled to demonstrate responsiveness.
Yet the warning already given remains decisive. When secondary causes
attempt to secure what belongs to grace, distortion follows. The machine
runs harder and the organism weakens. Holiness Planning does not reject
coordination. It refuses inversion. It insists that final causality
remain explicit. It protects efficient causality as divine. It
disciplines material causes through discernment. It does not ask whether
strategy is useful but whether strategy is subordinate.

Saint John Paul II's phrase returns with force. To place pastoral
planning under the heading of holiness is "a choice filled with
consequences" (NMI, 31). Those consequences are not abstract. They
require relinquishment. They require a slower pace. They require
willingness to accept smaller visible results in exchange for deeper
fidelity. The Church cannot inhabit both logics equally. One will govern
the other. If strategic logic governs, holiness language becomes
decorative. If holiness logic governs, strategy becomes servant. The
matter is not stylistic but theological. Only when holiness has been
clarified as final cause, grace restored as primary efficient cause,
prayer protected, and discernment embraced can the Church evaluate her
structures without confusing means for ends.
