---
title: "Augustine and the Subtle Return of Pelagian Causality"

chapter_num: "Excursus"
---

The tension beneath pastoral over-management is not new. It is ancient.
It is theological. And it was named with precision by Saint Augustine in
his struggle against Pelagianism.

Pelagius did not deny grace verbally. He did something more subtle. He
affirmed grace while assigning decisive causal weight to the human will.
Grace became illumination, encouragement, assistance, but not the
primary interior cause of willing and doing the good.

Saint Augustine, commenting on Saint Paul's teaching about the Mosaic
Law, writes:

> "The law was given that grace might be sought; grace was given that
> the law might be fulfilled. For the law without grace makes men guilty
> by commanding what they cannot accomplish. But grace causes the law to
> be fulfilled, by imparting love through the Holy Spirit... Therefore
> we do not say that free will is taken away by grace, but that it is
> established by grace. For it is grace which heals the will, so that it
> may freely love righteousness"(*De Spiritu et Littera*, 32--33).

In its original context, Augustine is addressing the Law as command
apart from the interior gift of the Spirit. The Law instructs; it
reveals what righteousness requires. But it does not itself impart the
love by which righteousness is accomplished. Grace alone heals the will
and enables what the command demands.

The Church's pastoral structures are not the Mosaic covenant, nor are
they opposed to grace. Yet Augustine's causal principle applies
analogically. Any structure, however well designed, that is treated as
causally productive of fruit apart from sustained dependence upon grace
begins to function like "law without Spirit." It commands, organizes,
and directs, but it does not generate charity. It can clarify
expectations, but not create holiness.

The issue, therefore, is not whether planning is legitimate. The issue
is causal order. Law in Augustine's sense reveals; grace fulfills.
Planning may reveal priorities, coordinate efforts, and articulate
goals. But only grace heals the will and produces the love by which
those goals are truly realized. When this order is maintained,
structures serve. When it is inverted, structures strain under a weight
they were never meant to carry.

Augustine insists elsewhere:

> "It is certain that we will when we will; but He brings it about that
> we will good... It is certain that we act when we act; but He brings
> it about that we act, by giving most effectual strength to the will...
> For the human will does not obtain grace by freedom, but obtains
> freedom by grace" (*De Natura et Gratia*, 26--31).

Here Augustine closes the causal loop. The will is real. Human action is
real. Their goodness, however, is not self-originating. The will does
not secure grace by its vigilance. Grace secures the will by healing its
interior weakness. Without this healing, even noble effort curves
inward.

In *The City of God*, Augustine describes the deeper anthropology:

> "Two loves have made two cities: the love of self unto the contempt of
> God, and the love of God unto the contempt of self" (*De Civitate
> Dei*, XIV.28).

The problem is not activity. The problem is orientation. The wounded
will seeks to secure what must be received. It prefers control to
dependence and measurable exertion to hidden surrender. It prefers to
guarantee fruitfulness rather than to abide.

This is why Pelagianism is so subtle. It can operate beneath orthodox
language. Grace may be affirmed verbally while being treated practically
as supplementary. Prayer becomes preparatory rather than causal.
Discernment becomes confirmation of one's already determined course
rather than receptive listening.

Augustine would recognize this pattern. For him, grace is not background
support for ecclesial competence. Grace is the interior cause of every
genuinely good act. As he states in *On the Predestination of the
Saints*:

> "God does not choose us because we believe, but that we may believe.
> Nor because we are holy, but that we may be holy" (*De
> Praedestinatione Sanctorum*, 2).

God's action is not reactive endorsement. It is generative causality.
The pastoral implication is unavoidable.

When stewards function, even implicitly, as though vigilance secures
fruitfulness, the logic of law has quietly replaced the logic of grace.
Structures and initiatives are then multiplied and metrics are used to
refine and evaluate. Yet with all this activity anxiety increases rather
than peace.

Augustine would say this is not accidental. Where the will assumes
primary causality, burden follows. The law without grace produces either
pride or despair: pride when apparent success appears to confirm one's
sufficiency, despair when outcomes resist control.

Grace alone produces both obedience and peace and grace does not bypass
the will. Grace heals the will and establishes it, making obedience
possible precisely by freeing it from the illusion of self-sufficiency.

Thus Augustine's famous prayer in the *Confessions* becomes not
devotional sentiment but theological clarity:

*"Give what You command, and command what You will" (Confessions,
X.29).*

The command remains. The mission remains. The shepherding responsibility
remains. But with the causal order restored, peace is restored because
what God commands, He must interiorly cause. Without that restoration,
even ecclesial zeal risks becoming what Augustine feared most: a
baptized form of self-reliance. With that restoration, obedience becomes
light. Planning becomes humble. Discernment becomes listening and fruit
becomes gift rather than achievement.

The issue, then, is not whether pastoral stewards should plan. The
Church has always ordered her life wisely. The issue is whether planning
is treated as instrumental or as generative, i.e., whether grace is
primary cause or supportive atmosphere.

Augustine would insist: where grace is primary, freedom flourishes.
Where the will assumes primacy, even holiness becomes strained. The
question is not whether we affirm grace. It is whether we assign it its
proper causality.

This Augustinian clarity stands in striking continuity with the summons
of Pope John Paul II in *Novo Millennio Ineunte*. When he insisted that
the Church must not rely on "pastoral initiatives" as though they were
decisive in themselves, but must instead "start afresh from Christ," he
was not offering devotional encouragement. He was restoring causality.
The command of Christ in Gospel of John 15, "Abide in me... for apart
from me you can do nothing" is not metaphorical spirituality but
ontological dependence. Augustine's anti-Pelagian insistence that grace
heals and moves the will so that it may freely love righteousness is
simply the interior articulation of that same Johannine logic. The vine
does not assist already-fruitful branches; it is the living source from
which fruit is possible at all. Where pastoral practice treats Christ as
inspirational rather than generative, the displacement Augustine fought
reappears in new dress. Where abiding is restored as primary, fruit
returns as gift.
