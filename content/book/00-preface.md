At the dawn of the third Christian millennium, the Holy Spirit spoke
clearly to the Church. He spoke through to a Church desperately in need
of Him. He spoke to a Church suffering significant decline in the West;
and to a culture gripped in an existential crisis. The Holy Spirit spoke
through a man that had witnessed this crisis develop through the
aftermath of two world wars, rampant secular humanism and who had
witnessed the Smoke of Satan in the world and in the Church. The Holy
Spirit spoke through St Pope John Paul II.

I write this book in the hope of retrieving to the current time and the
current milieu John Paul II's prophetic voice. In particular I hope to
make clear and applicable John Paul II's path to renewal he offered at
the close of the year 2000. This path was the fruit of John Paul II's
entire papacy. From *Redemptor Hominis* through the Great Jubilee
celebration, John Paul was fixed on guiding the Church into the third
Christian millennium on a path that would unite her more closely, more
intimately, more passionately with her Savior. *Novo Millennion Ineunte*
is that path.

St John Paul did not provide a new teaching. He did not provide a new
path or a new program. John Paul retrieved the wisdom of the Church
given to her by Jesus Christ, inscribed onto pages in Holy Scripture,
entrusted to the Successors of the Apostles in Sacred Tradition, lived
by the Saints and confirmed by the Magisterium. St John Paul II offered
the path of union with God, the path of the Saints; he offered the path
of holiness. What does that path look like today?

In every age the Church faces some form of crisis. No doubt the Church
is in another crisis. She has weathered previous crisis and will, in one
way or another, weather this one. It will likely require some pruning.
Jesus tells the Apostles the Father prunes vines that produce some fruit
so that they may produce more, and He removes those vines that do not
bear fruit. There have been 'prunings' in every age. The ancient martyrs
were 'prunings' that produced an abundant crop. The Protestant revolt
cut deeply, and the Church flourished afterwards. The decline in the
Church today has likewise the opportunity to bear abundant fruit.

There are signs here and there of renewal. These are small green buds in
response to pruning, but there remains greater confusion, division,
apathy and treachery that portends more cuts to come. Those cuts must
come for God wills that we be fruitful. The path to holiness John Paul
proposed has not yet been trod. Until we trod that path, individually
and collectively, the Church will continue her bitter descent.

The data shows broad decline in the practice of the Faith already at the
beginning of the last century. This may come as a surprise. It will
likely shock you. It undercuts the easy scapegoat that the decline that
is now visible was the fault of the Second Vatican Council. It also
takes off the table the easy answer that if we just return to the
practices of the Church before the Council all shall be well. If the
practices of the Church in the first half of the last century were
already in place as the Church's inner life crumbled and they did not
stem the tide then, how will they turn back the tsunami now? If this is
true, then what can be done going forward?

This is precisely where John Paul's retrieval of the path to holiness
breaks in. It is new because it has not been seen in recent decades, or
centuries, except in the Saints. Yet, it is the path we are all called
to walk. It is the path of the universal call to holiness of the Second
Vatican Council. It is the path of union with God that Jesus announces
to the Apostles before He leaves the Upper Room to die for you and for
me. It is the path of receptivity of God's word, like Mary's *fiat*. It
is the "thy will be done" of the Our Father. It is Jesus own,
"Father...thy will be done."

Using Scripture, the Saints and the Magisterium as anchors and guides,
this book seeks to make application of John Paul II's path clear and
simple, not easy. The easy way is rarely the fruitful one. That is as
true in daily life as it is in the spiritual life. Yet, the path is
truly walkable, if narrow and hard. The fruit will follow, as Jesus
promises.

It is the pastors of the Church who bear the greatest burden in ordering
the life of the Church to follow the path of renewal so it may bear
fruit. Certainly, each person must answer for their own soul and how
they answered the Holy Spirit's call. Yet, a steward is entrusted by
Jesus with His bride. As a pastor of souls, you will have to answer for
many souls; many whom you don't know and will never meet. What will your
response be to the Holy Spirit's call?

The hope of this book is not dramatic reform. It is a quiet reordering.
It is the restoration of proportion between effort and grace, between
structure and sanctity, between authority and fatherhood. If this
reordering occurs, renewal will follow. Not because we have mastered
technique, but because we have remembered Who acts. The question is not
whether the Church will act. She always does. The question is whether
she will act with metaphysical certainty according to her own causes. If
you are willing to examine that question openly and without haste, then
read on.
