---
title: "Stone and Splinters: Glory, Beauty, and the Causal Order of the Mass"
number: 10
weight: 10
summary: "Applies the causal logic to liturgy, clarifying principal and secondary causes and preventing aesthetic debates from replacing holiness as the end."
---

Imagine a solemn High Mass rising beneath the vaults of Chartres Cathedral, where incense ascends and chant unfolds with measured gravity. Gold, stone, and stained glass proclaim the majesty of God. Imagine again a whispered Mass offered in a wooden barrack at Auschwitz concentration camp by Maximilian Kolbe. There is no organ and no ceremonial splendor, only cold, fear, and the nearness of death. Which Mass gives more glory to God? The question forces clarity, and clarity requires hierarchy.

The Mass is holy because Jesus Christ acts. He is not remembered as a distant figure, nor invoked as a symbol, but present as the principal agent. The sacrament operates *ex opere operato*, not because of the fervor of the minister or the splendor of the rite, but because He Himself acts through it. The holiness of the Mass flows from His agency, not from the accidents that surround it, whether voice or silence, chant or whisper, vestment or bare wood. Ceremony may frame the action, but it does not generate it. The source is Jesus Christ.

More precisely still, the efficient cause of sanctification is divine grace. Grace is not an atmosphere or a sentiment. It is the participation in the very life of God, the power by which the soul is elevated beyond its natural capacity and made capable of communion with Him. Grace produces real change. It heals, strengthens, illumines, and unites. It is the power that sanctifies.

The sacrament is not identical with that grace. It is an instrumental efficient cause, a true channel through which grace is communicated. As an instrument in the hands of the principal agent (Christ), it does not act independently, nor does it substitute for the power it conveys. Through valid matter, form, and intention, the same Sacrifice of Calvary is made present and the grace flowing from that Sacrifice is truly conferred. The instrument does not create the life it carries; it transmits it. Recognition of this reality protects the discussion from emotionalism on either side and restores the center to its proper place.

Thomas Aquinas provides the metaphysical grounding necessary for such clarity. God is pure act. He lacks nothing and cannot be increased or perfected by creaturely action. When Christians speak of giving glory to God, they do not enlarge His intrinsic glory. They manifest it and participate in it. The increase occurs in the creature, not in God. Sacred beauty therefore does not make God more glorious; it manifests His glory fittingly to embodied worshippers. For Aquinas, the fittingness of worship arises from the nature of the human person. Man is an embodied knower. The senses mediate understanding. Ritual shapes the imagination. Harmony disposes the will toward what is good. The cathedral exists not because God requires marble, but because man requires mediation. Such a claim does not diminish solemn worship. It situates it within an anthropology that is both realistic and reverent.

Aquinas is equally clear that exterior worship is ordered to interior devotion. In the *Summa Theologiae* he teaches that exterior acts of religion are directed to the interior worship of the heart as sign and incentive. He further insists that the essence of sacrifice consists chiefly in the inward offering of the soul. Exterior rites are real and necessary for embodied creatures, yet they are ordered toward a higher reality, the movement of the heart toward God in love. This hierarchy is not optional. The exterior is subordinate to the interior, and the sign exists for the sake of the reality it signifies.

Teresa of Avila speaks directly to the confusion that often arises when senses and holiness are conflated. In The Interior Castle she repeatedly warns that consolations, even intense spiritual sweetness, are not reliable signs of sanctity. They may be gifts from God. They may also arise from temperament or imagination. What determines growth is not warmth, tears, awe, or felt closeness, but the increase of humility, obedience, perseverance, and, especially, charity. In Thomistic terms she distinguishes between accidental consolations and the formal growth of charity in the soul. Consolation is not perfection. A sense of reverence is not union. Holiness consists in loving God and neighbor and in doing His will when prayer feels dry as well as when it feels luminous.

Such distinctions are decisive for the contemporary liturgical debate. Aesthetic elevation can produce feelings of awe, tenderness, reverence, and nostalgia. These responses are not inherently suspect. They may dispose the soul toward recollection. Yet they are not the formal cause of holiness. A soul may be deeply moved in a cathedral and remain unchanged in charity. Another may experience dryness, distraction, or interior desolation and yet grow profoundly through fidelity. Prayer itself, understood properly, is not an emotion but an act of intellect *and* will under grace. It is a true secondary efficient cause in the order of cooperation, a real participation in God's drawing of the soul toward Himself. Emotional experience is neither identical with prayer nor equivalent to holiness.

Secondary efficient dispositive causes such as incense, chant, architecture, and ceremonial form assist the senses and help dispose the imagination. They quiet distractions and elevate perception. They are pedagogical and medicinal for fragile and distracted minds. They help prepare the soil of the soul in which grace bears fruit, but they do not produce grace in themselves. The form of holiness in the soul is charity. Aquinas and Teresa converge in affirming that interior transformation, not exterior impression, is primary. Confusion arises when stirring of the emotions is mistaken for growth in love. Such confusion can occur in solemnity and in simplicity alike.

The barracks of Auschwitz reveal the hierarchy with stark clarity. Nearly every aesthetic support was stripped away. There was no stained glass to lift the gaze and no ceremonial stability to structure the senses. There were no vestments or golden vessels. For some prisoners, distraction was burned away by proximity to death. Kolbe's charity, formed through years of fidelity, did not depend upon aesthetic consolation. His sanctity was not the product of atmosphere but of conformity to Jesus Christ. Teresa would recognize in such deprivation the purification she describes in the dark night, where attachment to sweetness is severed so that love may be purified. Auschwitz does not demonstrate the superiority of ugliness. It clarifies causality. When every consolation is removed, and every secondary dispositive cause of chant, incense, etc. is stripped away, what is left? Love alone remains. God alone remains.

Pastoral realism requires balance. The Church rightly seeks beautiful worship because most souls are not living in martyrdom. They are distracted, tired, and wounded. They require the mediation that beauty offers. Exterior splendor, ordered properly, can assist recollection and strengthen devotion. The danger arises when beauty becomes the emotional guarantee of holiness. A parishioner may say that he felt close to God. That feeling may be a grace, or a temperament or an aesthetic preference. Holiness is measured by increased charity, patience, humility, obedience, and sacrifice. The same correction applies to debates concerning the Tridentine Mass and the Novus Ordo Mass. Arguments frequently revolve around experience, reverence, accessibility, transcendence, and communal warmth. The deeper Thomistic and Teresian question concerns charity. Does the celebration foster love of God and neighbor? Does it form the soul in sacrifice? Does it conform the worshipper to Christ?

Solemnity that fosters pride fails in its purpose. Simplicity that fosters casualness likewise fails. Beauty that becomes nostalgia fails. Austerity that becomes ideology fails. The rite is *not* the final cause. Union with God in charity is the final cause. Sacred beauty is ordered toward that participation. It serves the growth of charity but does not generate it. To fallen human perception the Cross was not beautiful, yet in obedience and love it was perfect glory. The measure of glory is conformity to the divine will.

Beneath vaulted stone or splintered wood, the essential question remains unchanged. Are we offering ourselves with Him? When the answer is yes, God is glorified, not because His being has increased, but because a creature has entered more deeply into His life. Such clarity does not diminish solemn worship. It protects it from misunderstanding and restores it to its proper place within the hierarchy of causes. In that restoration, light replaces heat and peace becomes possible within the discussion.
