When sacramental participation is examined on a per-capita basis, the
long arc of decline in the United States appears not as an abrupt
post-conciliar rupture, but as the maturation of much older conditions.
The relative stability of infant baptism between 1920 and 1960 must be
read in light of the postwar Baby Boom, when elevated Catholic fertility
rates should have exerted upward pressure on per-capita reception; that
such an increase does not materialize suggests that favorable
demographics were already compensating for a weakening interior
religious life. This dynamic was anticipated well before the twentieth
century. In the nineteenth century, John Henry Newman warned repeatedly
of a Christianity sustained by habit, respectability, and moral
sentiment rather than conviction, describing a "religion of the day"
capable of outward continuity even as inward seriousness diminished
(Parochial and Plain Sermons; Lectures on the Present Position of
Catholics in England, 1851). A century later, Joseph Ratzinger diagnosed
the same condition in explicitly ecclesial terms, arguing that modern
Europe had become a "Church of pagans who still call themselves
Christians," sustained by sacramental custom and cultural inheritance
rather than lived faith ("The New Pagans and the Church," 1958). The
modest sacramental bulge produced as the Baby Boom cohort passed through
childhood and adolescence in the 1960s--1980s thus reflects demographic
momentum passing through an already thinned ecclesial structure, not a
renewal of belief. Read together, the data, Newman's nineteenth-century
warnings, and Ratzinger's pre-conciliar analysis converge on the same
conclusion: long before institutional crises became visible, sacramental
practice was being sustained more by population and culture than by
deep, transmissible faith.
