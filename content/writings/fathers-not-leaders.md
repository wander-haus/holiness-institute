---
title: "Fathers, Not Leaders"
subtitle: "Holiness, Authority, and the Crisis of Ecclesial Imitation"
number: 7
weight: 7
summary: "Reframes authority in terms of fatherhood rather than leadership and grounds governance in holiness, arguing the Church needs fathers who are saints, not better leaders."
---

The contemporary Church speaks frequently of leadership. Dioceses seek leaders, seminaries promise to form leaders. Apostolates promise to form priests into superior administrators. Pastoral plans presume that leadership will secure renewal. This fixation, however, rests on a fundamental theological error. Leadership, even when informed and elevated by the virtues, belongs to the natural order. It can become effective in humanity's work in the world. It does not follow, however, that leadership is efficacious in the work of the Church, whose mission is spiritual and supernatural. Leadership is not a path to holiness. When elevated to a governing ecclesial ideal, it becomes a poor simulacrum of holiness. What the Church needs is not leaders, but fathers.

*Lumen gentium* decisively resists the reduction of the Church to an organization in need of better management. The Church is "in Christ like a sacrament" (*Lumen gentium*, 1), a mystery rooted in divine life, not a human project ordered primarily toward effectiveness. Her vitality does not arise from strategy or coordination, but from sanctification. Consequently, the Council places at the heart of its ecclesiology the universal call to holiness (cf. LG 39--42). Holiness is not instrumental to the Church's mission; it *is* the mission. Every office, ministry, and charism exists only insofar as it serves this supernatural end.

Yet modern ecclesial practice often reverses this hierarchy. Leadership becomes primary; holiness becomes presumed. Priests are increasingly evaluated less as spiritual fathers than as institutional leaders. Their success is measured by administrative competence, managerial skill, and strategic vision. This shift is not merely pastoral---it is theological. It quietly redefines priesthood according to categories alien to its sacramental nature.

Saint John Paul II's *Novo millennio ineunte* stands as a direct challenge to this redefinition. At a moment when the Church might have been expected to unveil a comprehensive plan for the future, the Pope instead refuses the logic of programmatic renewal.

> "It is not therefore a matter of inventing a 'new programme'. The programme already exists: it is the plan found in the Gospel and in the living Tradition, it is the same as ever. Ultimately, it has its centre in Christ himself, who is to be known, loved and imitated, so that in him we may live the life of the Trinity, and with him transform history until its fulfilment in the heavenly Jerusalem" (*NMI*, 29).

The future of the Church does not depend on leadership innovation but on conformity to Christ. Before strategies, before plans, before leadership development, the Church must become a "school of holiness" (NMI, 43) "distinguished above all in the art of prayer" (NMI, 32).

This insistence exposes the poverty of leadership as a salvific category. Leadership promises control; holiness requires surrender. Leadership seeks influence; holiness bears fruit through self-emptying. Leadership operates within the natural order of cause and effect; holiness participates in the supernatural economy of grace. To confuse the two is to expect from leadership what only sanctity can give.

This confusion becomes especially acute when priests are absorbed into administrative leadership roles. To assign a priest to significant administrative duties is not merely inefficient; it is a misallocation of sacerdotal power. The priest is ordained not to manage institutions but to act *in persona Christi*, to offer sacrifice, forgive sins, celebrate the Sacraments, and preach the Word. His power is sacramental before it is functional. When a priest's primary energy is consumed by administration, the Church effectively treats the priesthood as a leadership role rather than a participation in Christ's own priesthood.

This misallocation is especially striking in light of Saint Paul's teaching on charisms. In Corinthians, Paul enumerates the gifts given to the Church:

> "first apostles, secondly prophets, thirdly teachers; after that miracles, then gifts of healing, services of help, power of administration and the speaking of various tongues" (1 Cor 12:28).

Significantly, administration appears late in the list. Paul does not deny its value; administration is indeed a charism. Yet, Paul then says, "strive after the greater gifts" (v 31). This placement is instructive. Administration is not foundational, not primary, and not constitutive of the Church's identity. The Church can survive poor administration or without the gift of tongues. She cannot survive without apostles; without holiness, truth, and sacramental life.

Paul's hierarchy of charisms reveals a logic largely ignored in contemporary ecclesial structures. Administration serves the Church; it does not define her. It supports the mission; it does not generate it. When administrative competence becomes the dominant expectation placed upon priests, the Church implicitly elevates a lesser charism above greater ones. Worse still, she assigns that lesser charism to those whose vocation is sacramental fatherhood, not organizational leadership.

The Church does not primarily need leaders because she does not primarily need coordination. She needs fathers because she needs life. Fatherhood is not a leadership style; it is a theological reality rooted in imitation of the heavenly Father. Fathers generate, protect, correct, and remain. They do not lead from a distance; they abide. Their authority is not derived from influence but from responsibility born of love.

*Lumen gentium* consistently frames ecclesial authority in paternal and pastoral terms, not managerial ones. Bishops and priests are shepherds configured to Christ, not executives appointed for effectiveness (LG 21, 28). Their authority is credible only insofar as it is cruciform and transparent to holiness. A father may be administratively weak and yet spiritually fruitful. A leader may be administratively strong and yet spiritually barren.

Leadership becomes a poor simulacrum of holiness because it imitates holiness's external features---authority, imitation, visibility---while evacuating its interior substance. Holiness conforms the person to Christ crucified. Leadership often conforms the Church to the world's standards of success. Leadership demands results; fatherhood demands fidelity. Leadership seeks admiration; fatherhood pours itself out.

The saints reveal how little the Church ultimately needs leadership. Benedict withdrew. Francis embraced poverty. Teresa focused on the interior life. Therese remained hidden. Mother Teresa helped the least among us. None of them *led* institutions in the modern sense. All of them became spiritual fathers and mothers whose fecundity far outstripped any strategic plan. They did not optimize systems; they gave life.

This is not an argument against prudence, order, or the legitimate exercise of administration. It is an argument for theological clarity. Administration is a gift---but a lesser one able to be exercised by anyone. Leadership may assist the Church---but it cannot sanctify her. Priests are not ordained to be leaders; they are ordained to be fathers. When the Church asks them to become what they are not, she deprives herself of what only they can give.

The crisis of the Church is not a leadership crisis. It is a crisis of misplaced priorities and distorted imitation. Leaders imitate other leaders. Fathers imitate the heavenly Father and draw their strength and station from Him. *Lumen gentium* and *Novo millennio ineunte* converge on this supernatural truth: the Church will not be renewed by better leadership, but by deeper holiness. Leadership is not a path to holiness. Fatherhood---configured to Christ and ordered toward sanctification---is. The Church does not need more leaders. She needs fathers who are saints.
