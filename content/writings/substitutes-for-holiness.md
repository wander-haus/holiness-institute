It is traditionally recounted that in 1273 a Dominican friar overheard
Our Lord speak to Saint Thomas Aquinas from the crucifix: "You have
written well of Me, Thomas; what reward will you have?" Thomas replied
simply, "Nothing but You, Lord." Later that same year, after a profound
mystical experience, he refused to continue dictating the Summa. To his
companion Reginald he said, "Everything that I have written seems like
straw to me compared to what I have seen and what has been revealed to
me."

## Substitutes for God

Those words do not diminish theology. They place it in its proper order.
Thomas did not repent of truth. He confessed that truth culminates in
encounter. The one who had spent his life articulating the mysteries of
God discovered anew that the end of all theology is not explanation but
possession. Not ideas but communion. Not argument but union.

The Church speaks with the same clarity about her own purpose. The
Catechism teaches, "In the Church this communion of men with God, in the
'love [that] never ends,' is the purpose which governs everything in
her that is a sacramental means, tied to this passing world. ...
Holiness is measured according to the 'great mystery' in which the Bride
responds with the gift of love to the gift of the Bridegroom" (CCC 773).
Communion in love is the governing purpose. Everything else is
sacramental means.

Saint John Paul II states it with pastoral urgency in Novo Millennio
Ineunte: "First of all, I have no hesitation in saying that all pastoral
initiatives must be set in relation to holiness" (NMI 30). He
immediately grounds this in Scripture: "This is the will of God, your
sanctification" (1 Thess 4:3). Holiness is not a specialized path for a
few. It is the destiny of all the baptized.

Yet many faithful Catholics, sincerely devoted and active in parish
life, have absorbed subtle substitutes. These substitutes are not evil.
They are often good. They are sometimes necessary. They become
distortions when they are mistaken for the end rather than ordered to
it. The restless heart that Saint Augustine described does not find rest
in fragments. "You have made us for Yourself, O Lord, and our heart is
restless until it rests in You" (Confessions I, 1). If we enthrone
something less than God as our highest good, restlessness follows, even
if that something bears the appearance of religion.

## The Restless Heart and the One Thing Necessary

Christ does not leave the matter vague. "You, therefore, must be
perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect" (Mt 5:48). That perfection
is not omnipotence or omniscience. "God is love" (1 Jn 4:8). The
perfection to which we are called is charity. When Martha is "anxious
and troubled about many things," Jesus replies, "One thing is necessary"
(Lk 10:42). The Christian life can become crowded with good works,
devotions, and concerns. Yet only one thing is necessary. Mary sits at
His feet.

Saint Thomas examines the most obvious substitutes in the Summa Contra
Gentiles. Wealth cannot satisfy. "It is impossible for man's happiness
to consist in wealth" (SCG III, c. 33). Nor can honor, nor fame, nor
power. These goods are finite. The human heart is ordered to the
infinite. Christ Himself warns, "What will it profit a man, if he gains
the whole world and forfeits his soul?" (Mt 16:26). The restlessness
Augustine names is not cured by accumulation. It is cured by union.

The substitutes we now examine arise not from worldliness but from
partial truths elevated to ultimacy. They are more dangerous precisely
because they are near the truth.

## Intellectualism and Orthodoxism

The first substitute is the reduction of holiness to correct belief.
Orthodoxy matters. The Church defends dogma because truth saves. Yet the
tradition itself warns that knowledge about God is not yet possession of
God.

Saint Thomas writes, "Happiness does not consist in the knowledge of God
by faith" (SCG III, c. 40). Faith is real knowledge. It adheres to
divine authority. Yet it remains mediated and obscure. Saint Paul says,
"For now we see in a mirror dimly, but then face to face" (1 Cor 13:12).
Faith prepares for vision, but it is not yet vision.

Saint James delivers a sobering line: "You believe that God is one; you
do well. Even the demons believe---and shudder" (James 2:19). Correct
belief alone does not sanctify. It must be animated by love. Saint Paul
intensifies the point: "If I understand all mysteries and all knowledge
... but have not love, I am nothing" (1 Cor 13:2).

Christ rebukes those who mistake study for surrender: "You search the
scriptures, because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it
is they that bear witness to me; yet you refuse to come to me that you
may have life" (Jn 5:39--40). The Scriptures bear witness to Him. They
are not a substitute for Him.

Saint Teresa of Avila testifies to a knowledge beyond study. In her Life
she writes, "It is as if one without ever learning, without taking the
pains even to learn to read, and without studying any subject whatever,
should find himself in possession of all knowledge ... the mystery of
the most Holy Trinity so clearly revealed to it." She insists that no
theologian could dispute the clarity given in that moment (Life 27).
This is not anti-intellectualism. It is the confession that God must
communicate Himself.

Saint John Paul II, reflecting on Saint John of the Cross, writes that
his teaching "is the fruit of experience. It was a vital experience of
the supernatural reality that is communicated to the soul, a dynamic
experience of participation in the intimate life of the Blessed Trinity,
and, finally, an experience of the unifying power of that which serves
as a 'means of union' with God" (Dissertation on St John of the Cross,
n. 22). Participation in Trinitarian life is the goal.

John of St Thomas clarifies the distinction further. He teaches that the
intimate knowledge of God that transforms the soul does not arise from
discursive reasoning but from the Holy Spirit's infused light. Faith
places us in contact with divine truth, but charity unites us to the
divine life. The intellect can grasp propositions. Only love grasps God
Himself.

Saint Faustina, unlearned in theology, records in her Diary that God
allowed her to understand mysteries that she could never have explained
academically. She repeatedly calls herself ignorant, yet speaks with
luminous simplicity about the mercy of God and the interior life of the
Trinity. Union is gift.

The Catechism states, "The desire for God is written in the human heart"
(CCC 27). Not merely the desire to be correct about God. The desire for
God. Saint John Paul II pleads, "Our Christian communities must become
genuine 'schools' of prayer" (Novo Millennio Ineunte 34). Theology that
does not culminate in prayer risks becoming straw.

## Virtuism and Moral Achievement

The second substitute is the identification of holiness with moral
rectitude or visible virtue. Virtue is indispensable. Without it, the
spiritual life collapses. Yet virtue is ordered to love.

Saint Thomas writes, "Happiness is not in an act of virtue" (SCG III, c.
34). Acts of virtue dispose us for happiness. They do not constitute it.
Saint Paul states plainly, "If I give away all I have ... but have not
love, I gain nothing" (1 Cor 13:3). The absence of charity empties even
heroic acts.

Christ's warning to the Pharisees is severe: "You clean the outside of
the cup and of the plate, but inside they are full of greed and
self-indulgence" (Mt 23:25). External compliance can mask interior
poverty. Saint Augustine writes, "He loves Thee too little who loves
anything together with Thee which he loves not for Thy sake"
(Confessions X, 29). Even good acts must be ordered to God.

The Catechism teaches, "Charity is the theological virtue by which we
love God above all things for his own sake" (CCC 1822). Virtue finds its
perfection in charity. Saint Teresa observes that in deeper prayer, "the
Lord seems to make the virtues grow" (Life XVII, 4). Heroic virtue is a
fruit of union, not a self-generated achievement.

Moralism tempts the laity because daily life offers constant
opportunities to measure goodness. Yet Christ does not call us merely to
decency. He calls us to share in His own filial love for the Father. "Be
merciful, even as your Father is merciful" (Lk 6:36). Mercy is not
minimal compliance. It is participation in divine love.

## Liturgism and the Nature of the Sacraments

The third substitute concerns worship. The liturgy is central. Yet
attachment to form can eclipse substance.

The Council of Trent teaches definitively, "If anyone says that by the
sacraments of the New Law grace is not conferred ex opere operato ...
let him be anathema" (Session VII, Canon 8). Grace is conferred by
Christ's action, not by aesthetic perfection. In Session XXII, Trent
declares, "In this divine sacrifice ... the same Christ is contained and
immolated in an unbloody manner who once offered Himself in a bloody
manner on the altar of the cross." The identity of the sacrifice is
constant.

Saint John Chrysostom exhorts the faithful: "When you see the Lord
immolated and lying upon the altar ... think not that it is man who
causes this to be done" (Homily 82 on Matthew). Saint Augustine reminds
us in speaking of Baptism, "It is He who baptizes" (Tractates on John
6). Christ is the principal agent.

The sacraments are holy because Christ acts. The Church has known
various rites throughout her history. The underlying reality remains the
same. To imply that one valid celebration confers more Christ than
another is to misunderstand sacramental theology. Christ does not divide
Himself.

Jesus prays, "That they may all be one" (Jn 17:21). When liturgical
preference becomes a badge of superiority or division, unity is wounded.
Reverence matters. Beauty matters. Yet they serve love. The Eucharist is
Christ's gift of Himself. "The Eucharist makes the Church" (CCC 1396).
The focus is the Bridegroom, not the trappings.

## Activism and the Loss of Abiding

The fourth substitute is activism detached from contemplation. Love of
neighbor is essential. Christ identifies Himself with the least. Yet
action without union becomes brittle.

Pope Benedict XVI writes in Deus Caritas Est, "Being Christian is not
the result of an ethical choice or a lofty idea, but the encounter with
an event, a person" (DCE 1). That Person is Christ. Without encounter,
works become self-referential.

He continues, "Love of neighbour is thus shown to be possible ... It
consists in the very fact that, in God and with God, I love even the
person whom I do not like" (DCE 18). The phrase "in God and with God" is
decisive. Charity flows from abiding.

Christ says, "Abide in me, and I in you. ... Apart from me you can do
nothing" (Jn 15:4--5). Activism that neglects abiding loses its source.
Martha's anxiety is not condemned because service is evil, but because
service detached from listening becomes disordered.

Saint Teresa insists that love of neighbor increases as love of God
deepens. Saint Paul warns that without love, even sacrificial generosity
profits nothing (1 Cor 13:3). The reservoir must be filled before it can
overflow.

## Seeking Heaven Rather than God

The final substitute is the reduction of Christianity to securing
Heaven. Heaven is our destiny. Yet Christ commands love. "You shall love
the Lord your God with all your heart" (Mt 22:37). He does not command
calculation.

The Catechism teaches, "The beatitude we are promised confronts us with
decisive moral choices. It invites us to purify our hearts ... and to
seek the love of God above all else" (CCC 1723). Heaven is the fruit of
love.

Saint Paul exhorts maturity: "When I became a man, I gave up childish
ways" (1 Cor 13:11). To remain focused primarily on avoiding Hell is to
remain spiritually juvenile. Christ reveals the true end in His priestly
prayer: "That the love with which thou hast loved me may be in them, and
I in them" (Jn 17:26). The Christian is invited into the very
circulation of Trinitarian love.

## The Heights to Which We Are Called

The substitutes cannot satisfy the restless heart. None of them can fill
the ache Augustine names. Only unity with the Trinity can do that. "This
is eternal life, that they know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ
whom you have sent" (Jn 17:3). To know in this sense is to share life.

The Father did not send the Son so that we might be merely correct. The
Son did not shed His Blood so that we might be merely decent. The Spirit
was not poured out so that we might be merely active. "The love of God
has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been
given to us" (Rom 5:5). We are called to participate in that love.

"Our God is a consuming fire" (Heb 12:29). He does not invite us to
mediocrity. He invites us to union. Saint Peter writes that we are
called to "become partakers of the divine nature" (2 Pet 1:4). That is
the height.

At the end of life, when arguments fall silent and preferences fade,
only one question will remain. Did we abide. Did we love. Did we receive
and return the love poured into our hearts.

Saint Thomas, Doctor of the Church, who wrote with unmatched clarity,
left us with the simplest and most radical confession. Non nisi te,
Domine! Nothing but You, Lord.
