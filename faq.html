The Holiness Institute begins from a simple question that becomes less simple the longer one sits with it: if the Church exists to make saints, why are so many of her practical judgments governed by activity, management, growth, enrollment, revenue, and institutional preservation?

That question is not meant as a complaint. It is meant as an examination of conscience. St. John Paul II gave the Church a rule at the threshold of the third millennium: “All pastoral initiatives must be set in relation to holiness” (Novo Millennio Ineunte, 30). The purpose of this page is to answer ordinary questions about the Institute and to place before the reader the deeper questions that follow once holiness is taken seriously as the Church’s governing end.

Basic Questions

What is The Holiness Institute?

The Holiness Institute exists to help the Church recover the path St. John Paul II placed before her in Novo Millennio Ineunte: renewal in Jesus Christ through holiness, prayer, grace, sacramental life, and the witness of love.

The Institute is not an ecclesial bureaucracy, a strategic planning firm, or a devotional project in the narrow sense. It is an attempt to retrieve the Church’s own grammar of renewal. Jesus Christ is the center. Grace acts first. Holiness is the end. Prayer is the ordinary way the soul receives what God gives. The saints show that this path can actually be lived.

What does the Institute mean by holiness?

Holiness is union with God in the perfection of love. It is conformity to Jesus Christ by grace, so that the life of Christ becomes the interior principle of the believer’s life.

This matters because holiness is often reduced to nearby goods: moral seriousness, liturgical reverence, doctrinal precision, parish involvement, or external observance. These are real goods when ordered properly; however, none of them is holiness. Holiness is the life of the Trinity communicated to the soul and received in love. “God’s love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us” (Rom 5:5).

Go deeper: What Holiness Is

Why does the Institute begin with St. John Paul II?

At the close of the Great Jubilee, St. John Paul II did something remarkable. At a moment when many expected a new pastoral program, he refused the logic of novelty. “It is not therefore a matter of inventing a ‘new programme,’” he wrote. “The programme already exists” (Novo Millennio Ineunte, 29). That programme has its center in Christ Himself, “to be known, loved and imitated” (NMI, 29).

The Institute takes that seriously. If the programme already exists, the Church’s difficulty is not that she lacks sufficient technique. The deeper difficulty is that she has forgotten how to let the existing programme govern her life.

Is this against planning?

No. Planning is necessary, but planning must remain subordinate to, and ordered to, holiness, prayer, grace, sacramental fruitfulness, and charity.

The problem begins when planning quietly becomes the governing confidence of pastoral life. St. John Paul II warned that it is “fatal” to forget that without Christ we can do nothing (NMI, 38). This does not abolish intelligence, prudence, structures, budgets, or administration. It places them back where they belong. They can dispose and serve sanctification; they do not generate it.

What is Holiness Planning?

Holiness Planning is the ordering of pastoral life according to the Church’s true end: the sanctification of souls and union with God in love.

Strategic planning usually begins with institutional goals: growth, efficiency, retention, revenue, staffing, visibility, engagement, or measurable impact. Holiness Planning begins with final causality. What is the Church for? What is this parish, school, apostolate, seminary, or diocese for? Once the end is named, every structure is judged by whether it definitively serves that end.

The governing question becomes direct: does this deepen prayer, form saints, strengthen families, lead souls to the sacraments with living faith, and conform persons more deeply to Christ?

The Data

Why does the Institute say the decline began before the Second Vatican Council?

Because the per-capita sacramental data makes that conclusion inescapable.

Many Catholics assume the crisis began in the 1960s. The Second Vatican Council, liturgical change, weak catechesis, and cultural upheaval are often treated as the whole explanation. The data complicates that story. When infant baptisms, marriages, and receptions into full communion are measured per Catholic rather than only by raw totals, the weakening is already visible before the Council.

That matters. If the decline was already underway, then the Council did not strike down a healthy Catholic organism. The Council, and the upheavals surrounding it, exposed a weakness that had been developing for decades beneath demographic strength, ethnic cohesion, habit, and cultural support.

Go deeper: Interpreting the Sacramental Decline

Why use per-capita sacramental data?

Raw numbers can make an institution look healthy while its sacramental density is falling.

A growing Catholic population can produce more activity, more buildings, more schools, and more visible institutional presence, even while fewer Catholics, proportionally, are receiving the sacraments. Per-capita sacramental data asks a sharper question: compared with the Catholic population, how dense is sacramental life?

This is why the population line matters. When Catholic population rises while infant baptisms, marriages, and conversions per Catholic fall, a spiritual deficit opens. That widening space is something many faithful Catholics feel before they can name it. The Church appears busy, but something is missing.

What is the “delta” the Institute speaks about?

The delta is the widening gap between institutional size and sacramental reception.

A diocese can have active ministries, full calendars, strong appeals, polished communications, professional staff, and healthy accounts while sacramental participation per Catholic continues to fall. The gap between visible institutional activity and sacramental fruitfulness is the spiritual deficit. It is also the reason ordinary Catholics can sense that the Church is active without being deeply alive.

That gap should trouble pastors. Full pews, full programs, full schools, and full bank accounts can be encouraging signs; they can also conceal decline if they become the primary measures of success.

Go deeper: The Deficit Beneath the Activity

Is the Institute blaming Vatican II?

No. The Institute’s claim is almost the opposite of the usual argument.

The Second Vatican Council reaffirmed the universal call to holiness, the Church as sacrament, the dignity of the lay vocation, the domestic Church, and the sanctifying mission of the Church. The Council is not the enemy of holiness. The easier mistake is to blame the Council for a decline that had already begun, then imagine that returning to pre-conciliar forms would be sufficient.

If those forms were already present while the interior life weakened, they cannot be the whole remedy. The question is more searching: what was missing even when many exterior forms were intact?

Interior Life

What does the Institute mean by “substitutes for holiness”?

A substitute for holiness is a real Catholic good that begins to function as though it were holiness itself.

Orthodoxy can become a substitute when correct doctrine is treated as the whole of Christian life. Liturgy can become a substitute when reverent form does not lead to deeper self-gift. Moral seriousness can become a substitute when the Christian life is reduced to rule-keeping. Activism can become a substitute when effort is mistaken for fruitfulness. Institutional success can become a substitute when activity is measured more carefully than sanctity.

The danger lies in their nearness. Each substitute resembles holiness closely enough to deceive serious Catholics.

Is the interior life just Mass, confession, and the rosary?

Mass, confession, and the rosary are essential treasures of Catholic life. The difficulty begins when they are treated as a completed checklist rather than as privileged places of encounter with Jesus Christ.

A person can attend Mass, confess regularly, say the rosary, and still remain largely unformed in deep prayer, trust, abandonment, charity, and living union with God. The sacraments have not failed when this happens. The soul may never have been taught to receive their fruit deeply.

The interior life is the life of grace consciously received and lived. It includes the sacraments, vocal prayer, meditation, affective prayer, recollection, purification, abandonment, and self-gift. It moves from practice into communion. It teaches the soul to say, with real trust, “Father.”

What is the interior life?

The interior life is the life of grace consciously received, cultivated, and brought to maturity in the soul. It is the hidden life of the person with God: faith becoming trust, trust becoming abandonment, abandonment becoming self-gift, and self-gift becoming communion in love.

This life is not static or flat. A person does not simply “have” an interior life in the same way he has a schedule, a devotion, or a parish membership. The tradition speaks of growth through the Three Ways: the purgative way, the illuminative way, and the unitive way. In the purgative way, the soul is being cleansed of serious sin, disordered attachments, and the first illusions about God and self. In the illuminative way, the soul begins to live more steadily by the light of Christ, with deeper prayer, more stable virtue, and greater docility to grace. In the unitive way, love becomes simpler, deeper, more surrendered, and more conformed to Jesus Christ.

This progression matters because many Catholics have been given practices without being taught the path. They know that Mass matters, confession matters, the rosary matters, and Catholic doctrine matters. Yet they may not know how the soul matures in prayer, how God purifies love, how consolation differs from union, how dryness can be fruitful, or how the Holy Spirit gradually teaches the soul to yield. Consequently, the sacramental and devotional life can remain real but under-received, like rain falling on soil that has not been tilled deeply enough.

The saints do not describe the interior life as religious mood. They describe it as transformation. St. Teresa of Avila’s mansions, St. John of the Cross’s nights, St. Thérèse’s little way, and St. Catherine of Siena’s dialogue of love all witness to movement. God draws the soul inward so that He may send it outward in charity. The person becomes fruitful because he has first become receptive.

Can someone teach prayer if he only knows technique?

A person can teach the mechanics of prayer without being a master of prayer, just as a man can explain the rules of music without being able to play beautifully. The mechanics have their place. Catholics should be taught how to make a meditation, how to examine conscience, how to pray with Scripture, how to pray the rosary attentively, how to prepare for confession, how to adore Christ in the Eucharist, and how to remain faithful when prayer feels dry.

Technique alone cannot teach the deeper life of prayer.

Prayer is not merely a method. Prayer is living contact with God. It is the soul receiving the Father through the Son in the Holy Spirit. A teacher who knows only technique may produce disciplined religious activity; he cannot easily form a soul in trust, abandonment, purification, receptivity, and love unless he has begun to live those realities himself. Nemo dat quod non habet. No one gives what he does not have. Agere sequitur esse. Action follows being.

This is why the Church has always needed spiritual fathers and mothers, not merely instructors. The guide must know the terrain from within. He must be able to recognize when a soul is lazy and when it is being purified, when consolation is a gift and when it is being grasped, when zeal is charity and when it is self-importance wearing religious clothing. These judgments do not come from technique alone. They come from holiness, experience, doctrine, humility, and prayer.

The practical consequence is significant. A parish can offer many prayer programs and still fail to form people in prayer. A school can teach many prayers and still leave children unable to pray. A seminary can require spiritual direction and still produce men who know the vocabulary of the interior life without having surrendered to it. The Church does not need fewer practices. She needs guides who know where the practices are meant to lead.

Why are Mass, confession, and the rosary not enough by themselves?

Mass, confession, and the rosary are more than enough in themselves because Christ acts through the sacraments and teaches His people through the prayer of the Church. The problem is never the poverty of these gifts. The problem is the soul’s limited receptivity.

A Catholic can attend Mass without learning to offer himself with Christ. He can confess sins without yielding the deeper attachments that feed them. He can pray the rosary while remaining at the surface of the mysteries. These practices remain holy, powerful, and necessary. Yet their fruit depends in part on the soul’s willingness to be formed, purified, and drawn into love.

The interior life is what allows these gifts to penetrate. Mass becomes participation in Christ’s self-offering. Confession becomes conversion rather than maintenance. The rosary becomes contemplation of the face of Christ with Mary rather than repetition alone. The same practices remain; however, the soul begins to receive them differently because it is becoming different.

This is why the Institute insists that holiness must govern pastoral life. If the faithful are given practices without being taught the path of prayer, they may conclude that Catholic life is only obligation, identity, or habit. If they are taught the interior life, they can begin to see that every Catholic practice is ordered toward union with God.

What does the Institute mean by “prayer is causal”?

Prayer is not decoration placed around pastoral action. Prayer is the ordinary way the Church abides in Christ and receives the grace without which she can do nothing.

Jesus does not say, “Apart from Me you can do less.” He says, “Apart from Me you can do nothing” (Jn 15:5). Prayer is where the branch remains on the vine. It is where the priest, bishop, parent, catechist, and apostle receive before they act. Pastoral action without prayer may remain active for a time; it will not bear the fruit Christ promises.

Why doesn’t the Institute begin by “doing evangelization”?

Many Catholics can feel the urgency. Children are leaving the Church, parishes are aging, marriages are fewer, conversions are few, and the surrounding culture is no longer carrying even the remnants of Christian memory. The natural response is to do more evangelization: launch initiatives, train teams, improve messaging, develop outreach, build platforms, and increase activity.

The instinct is generous. The order is often backwards.

No one gives what he does not have: nemo dat quod non habet. Action follows being: agere sequitur esse. A parish that is not deeply living from Christ cannot give Christ convincingly by multiplying outreach. A family that does not know how to pray cannot transmit prayer by better religious language. A priest who is not abiding in Christ cannot father souls by becoming more active. Evangelization is not the Church’s attempt to compensate for a weak interior life. Evangelization is the overflow of a life already in communion with God.

This is why Jesus does not first tell the apostles to organize a campaign. He calls them “to be with Him” before He sends them out to preach (Mk 3:14). Presence comes before mission. Abiding comes before fruit. “Apart from Me you can do nothing” (Jn 15:5). When this order is reversed, the Church becomes busy with activity that has too little foundation, too little prayer, and too little living contact with God. The result may look energetic for a time; nevertheless, it cannot bear the fruit of holiness because it is trying to give what has not first been received.

Evangelization begins when a person, family, parish, priest, or bishop has truly encountered Jesus Christ and lives from that encounter. The Church does not evangelize by manufacturing religious energy. She evangelizes by bearing witness to the One she knows, loves, receives, and imitates.

Why can’t we generate communion first so that people will love God more?

Communion is one of the great marks of Christian life, and every parish longs for it. People want belonging, friendship, shared mission, mutual support, and a sense that they are part of something larger than themselves. These desires are good. Yet communion becomes fragile when it is pursued as a social product before it is received as a spiritual fruit.

Christian communion arises from common union with God. It is the fruit of shared charity, shared worship, shared truth, shared sacramental life, and shared love of the Father through Christ in the Holy Spirit. We do not first create communion so that we can then learn to love God. We love God, and that common love draws us into communion with one another.

The Church’s deepest unity is not produced by events, small groups, hospitality teams, parish branding, or shared projects. These may serve communion when the root is alive. They become substitutes when the root is weak. A parish can be socially active and spiritually disconnected. People can know one another’s names, attend the same programs, and serve on the same committees while lacking the unity that comes from a common life in Christ.

St. John tells us the order plainly: “If we walk in the light, as He is in the light, we have fellowship with one another” (1 Jn 1:7). Fellowship follows walking in the light. Communion follows holiness. The Church becomes one by abiding in the One who makes her one.

The practical consequence is severe but liberating. A parish seeking communion should begin with prayer, repentance, adoration, the Eucharist, confession, the works of mercy, and serious formation in charity. Social life then becomes the expression of a shared supernatural life. Without that foundation, communion becomes another name for activity, and activity without living contact with God eventually exhausts the people it was meant to gather.

What happens when prayer becomes decorative?

Prayer is often placed around pastoral action like a Catholic frame around decisions already made. A meeting opens with prayer, a plan is commended to God, a project is blessed, and then the work proceeds according to the same assumptions that would govern any natural organization. God is invoked, yet He is rarely allowed to govern.

There is another distortion close beside it. Prayer becomes a means of asking God to ratify what we have already chosen. We discern after deciding. We ask for blessing after selecting the path. We pray for success after defining success by institutional measures. In this form, prayer remains religious in vocabulary while becoming managerial in practice.

Christian prayer has a deeper order. It is the place where the Church receives before she acts. It is where the branch remains in the vine. “Apart from Me you can do nothing” (Jn 15:5). St. John Paul II therefore places prayer at the heart of pastoral fruitfulness, warning that plans come to nothing when the primacy of grace is forgotten (NMI, 38). Prayer is not a spiritual covering laid over human initiative. It is living contact with the God who acts first.

A parish, diocese, school, family, or apostolate that prays only after its own conclusions have been reached has already surrendered the order of grace. The question is no longer whether prayer is present. The question is whether prayer governs.

Why isn’t holiness a feeling or a mood?

Holiness is often imagined as a religious atmosphere: warmth, reverence, consolation, beauty, seriousness, peace, or emotional nearness to God. These may accompany holiness, and when they do, they should be received with gratitude. They are not holiness itself.

Holiness is union with God in the perfection of love. It is conformity to Jesus Christ by grace. It is the soul made capable of receiving the life of the Trinity and giving itself back in charity. This can feel peaceful. It can also feel dry, hidden, costly, and cruciform. The saints are witnesses precisely because holiness often matures when consolation disappears and love remains.

This matters pastorally because a Church that mistakes holiness for a mood will form people to seek experiences rather than conversion. She will confuse reverent atmosphere with sanctity, emotional intensity with prayer, and visible seriousness with self-gift. The result may look devout, yet the deeper question remains untouched: is the person becoming more like Christ?

Holiness is measured by love. “All the faithful of Christ…are called to the fullness of the Christian life and to the perfection of charity” (Lumen Gentium, 40). The Church should be grateful when beauty, consolation, and reverence help the soul receive grace. She should never confuse the fragrance of holiness with holiness itself.

What is the difference between activity and fruitfulness?

Activity is what the Church does. Fruitfulness is what God brings forth when the Church abides in Christ.

The distinction matters because activity can multiply while fruit diminishes. A parish can have more ministries and less prayer. A diocese can have more offices and less sacramental density. A school can have more programs and fewer saints. A family can maintain Catholic observance while failing to transmit a living relationship with Jesus Christ.

Fruitfulness is judged by holiness: deeper faith, fuller charity, stronger prayer, sacramental life, conversion, self-gift, spiritual maturity, and communion with God.

Family and Education

Why does the Institute speak so much about the family?

Because the family is not merely an object of parish care. It is a subject of ecclesial action.

The family is the domestic Church, the first seminary, the seminary of love, the first school of prayer, and the ordinary place where a child learns whether God can be trusted. Parents do not receive their educational and formative mission from a parish program. They receive it from the natural act of generation, elevated and strengthened by the sacrament of Matrimony.

This changes the whole pastoral imagination. The parish exists to strengthen the family’s mission, not to replace it. The Catholic school exists to assist parents, not to absorb their vocation. Family ministry should not be one diocesan department among others. The family is one of the primary human places where holiness is formed.

Why does the Church speak of the family with such dignity?

A family can be so ordinary that its mystery disappears from view. A husband and wife make promises, children are born, meals are prepared, correction is given, forgiveness is asked, work is endured, prayers are said, and love is learned slowly in the same rooms where laundry waits and tempers fail. Precisely because the family is so near, it is easy to reduce it to a social unit, a pastoral concern, an educational setting, or a demographic necessity.

Scripture reveals something deeper. “Male and female He created them” (Gen 1:27). Man is made in the image of God, and that image shines not only in the solitary person’s capacity for reason and love, but also in the communion of persons. “It is not good that the man should be alone” (Gen 2:18). From the beginning, the human person is created for communion, gift, fruitfulness, and shared life. Marriage and family therefore belong to the created grammar by which God reveals both what the person is and, analogically, who He is. Before Sinai, before the prophets, before the Incarnation, God had already inscribed into creation an original revelation of Himself: fatherhood, motherhood, sonship, spousal love, fruitfulness, and communion.

The New Testament raises this dignity into the light of Christ. At Cana, Jesus begins His signs in the presence of a wedding feast (Jn 2:1–11). In Nazareth, the eternal Son receives human life within a family, living under Mary and Joseph in hidden obedience (Lk 2:51–52). In Ephesians, St. Paul speaks of marriage through the mystery of Christ and the Church: “This mystery is a profound one” (Eph 5:32). The family is not merely a useful setting for Christian life. It is one of the created and sacramental forms through which divine love becomes visible in history.

The magisterium speaks accordingly. Lumen Gentium calls the family the “domestic church” (LG, 11). Familiaris Consortio says that the Christian family is “the first community called to announce the Gospel to the human person during growth” and to bring him or her toward full human and Christian maturity (FC, 2). The Catechism teaches that the Christian family is “a communion of persons, a sign and image of the communion of the Father and the Son in the Holy Spirit” (CCC, 2205). St. John Paul II says that man and woman, created as a “unity of the two,” are called to mirror “the communion of love that is in God” (Mulieris Dignitatem, 7).

This is why the family’s dignity cannot be measured by its usefulness to parish life. The family is not dignified because it supplies volunteers, future priests, tuition, enrollment, donors, or participants in programs. Its dignity is rooted in creation, elevated by Matrimony, and drawn into Christ’s spousal love for the Church. In the sacramental family, the communion of husband, wife, and child becomes a living icon of origin, relation, gift, reception, fruitfulness, and shared life.

This likeness must be purified by reverence. The family is not the Trinity, and no created communion can be carried into God without purification. Divine fatherhood, sonship, procession, and love exceed every creaturely image. Yet God Himself chose fatherhood, sonship, motherhood, bridegroom love, birth, household, and family as privileged forms of revelation. The Word became flesh in a family. That choice was not accidental. Divine wisdom judged the family fitting to receive the Son.

Consequently, the family is not one pastoral object among many. It is one of the Church’s primary places of formation, sanctification, communion, and mission. Parents are not assistants to the parish in the formation of their children. They are the first and foremost educators, and Familiaris Consortio teaches that their right and duty is “original and primary,” “irreplaceable and inalienable,” and therefore incapable of being entirely delegated or usurped (FC, 36).

A parish that understands the dignity of the family will therefore ask different questions. Does this program strengthen the domestic Church? Does this school serve the parents’ vocation? Does this schedule protect family prayer, meals, rest, and Sunday worship? Does this pastoral structure help fathers and mothers become saints? Does this ministry treat families as consumers of religious services or as living subjects of ecclesial mission?

The family is the first seminary of love. It is the place where the person first learns whether love is trustworthy, whether fatherhood is gift or threat, whether authority serves, whether forgiveness is possible, whether prayer belongs to life, and whether God can be approached as Father. If the Church wants renewal, she must recover the dignity of the family as God made it, Christ entered it, Matrimony elevates it, and the magisterium has never ceased to teach it.

What does subsidiarity have to do with Catholic education?

Subsidiarity protects the proper agency of the family.

Parents are the first and primary educators of their children. This is not sentimental language. It is doctrinal, juridical, and pastoral. Catholic schools, parish catechesis, youth programs, and diocesan initiatives can serve families beautifully; however, they become disordered when they quietly assume responsibilities that belong properly to parents.

The educational question should therefore be asked in a different way. Does this school, curriculum, program, or diocesan policy strengthen parents in their vocation, or does it reduce them to consumers, volunteers, funders, or logistical assistants?

Is the Institute against Catholic schools?

No. Catholic schools can be powerful instruments of formation when they remain rightly ordered.

The concern is causal inversion. A Catholic school serves best when it supports the family’s primary role, deepens sacramental life, strengthens prayer, and forms students toward Jesus Christ. It becomes fragile when it functions mainly as an institutional system to be preserved, especially if parents are treated as obstacles to enrollment, tuition, policy compliance, or diocesan planning.

The sharper question is not whether Catholic schools should exist. The sharper question is whether each Catholic school is forming saints and strengthening families as domestic churches.

Why doesn’t the Institute begin with politics or culture?

Many Catholics want to change the culture, and the desire is understandable. We can see the disorder in law, education, entertainment, public morality, family policy, and civic life. The temptation is to look for the lever that will move culture back into place.

There is no such lever.

Politics is downstream of culture, but culture is downstream of the family, and the family is downstream of the person. A culture is not an object that can be seized and moved by strategy. It is the slow outward expression of what persons love, what families live, what children receive, what communities honor, and what sins become normal enough to be defended.

This also means that cultural collapse is personal before it is political. A civilization does not usually abandon God all at once. It does so through countless individual abandonments of holiness: prayer cooled, marriages weakened, children unformed, fathers absent, mothers exhausted, pastors distracted, schools secularized in imagination even when Catholic in name, and families gradually losing confidence that grace is enough. Over time, those private losses become public facts.

Renewal follows the same path in reverse, though more slowly. The descent is usually quicker than the uphill climb. That should sober us rather than discourage us. If the family is the first seminary, the seminary of holiness, and if the person is the living place where grace is either received or resisted, then the renewal of culture begins with individual holiness. A holy person can form a holy family. Holy families can form a Catholic culture. A Catholic culture can sustain just politics.

The Church therefore cannot begin by asking how to regain influence. She must begin by asking how to form saints. “Seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness” (Mt 6:33). Everything else depends upon whether we believe that order.

Priests and Bishops

What is wrong with describing the priest as priest, prophet, and king?

Nothing is wrong with the biblical pattern of priest, prophet, and king. The difficulty is that, in practice, it can be flattened into a clerical job description: sacramental function, teaching function, governing function.

The Institute uses Victim, Icon, and Servant to recover the interior form hidden within those offices. The priestly office reaches its depth in self-offering. The prophetic office reaches its depth in making Christ visible. The kingly office reaches its depth in service ordered by charity. The priest images Christ most fully when he becomes sacrificial, transparent, and given away.

This is the opposite of clericalism. Clericalism turns sacred office into possession, privilege, distance, or control. Victim, Icon, and Servant turns sacred office back toward kenosis.

Go deeper: Victim, Icon, and Servant

Why say that priests sometimes seek refuge in administration?

Administration can become a hiding place for a wounded or overwhelmed priest.

A priest who is exhausted by spiritual fatherhood may retreat into tasks that can be completed: budgets, buildings, schedules, policies, meetings, emails, and reports. These things have to be done. Yet they can also protect him from the more vulnerable work of priesthood: prayer, presence, confession, spiritual direction, preaching from union with Christ, suffering with his people, and becoming a father to souls.

A priest can be busy in the parish while hiding from the altar’s demand that he become a victim with Christ. That is a painful sentence, and it should be received with compassion. It is also often true.

Why is it troubling when priests are sent for business training before spiritual theology?

There is a quiet irony in the contemporary Church that deserves to be named carefully. Priests are sometimes encouraged to pursue MBAs, degrees in nonprofit management, leadership certificates, or organizational training, while advanced formation in spiritual theology, prayer, discernment, ascetical theology, or the theology of sanctification receives far less practical urgency.

This does not mean that business knowledge is useless. Parishes have budgets, employees, buildings, insurance, schools, cemeteries, vendors, legal obligations, and real temporal responsibilities. These things require prudence and competence. A priest who understands them can avoid harm, ask better questions, and govern more responsibly.

The question is one of proportion and vocation. The priest is ordained to sanctify, teach, and govern in Christ. His irreplaceable work is not financial analysis, facilities management, human resources, or nonprofit administration. His irreplaceable work is priestly fatherhood: offering sacrifice, forgiving sins, preaching the Word, forming souls, teaching prayer, discerning spirits, accompanying suffering, and making Christ visible to His people.

That makes the educational pattern revealing. A priest can often find parishioners, deacons, finance council members, attorneys, accountants, executives, contractors, teachers, and nonprofit professionals who already possess expertise in temporal administration. He can and should draw upon their competence. The lay faithful have gifts given for the building up of the Body of Christ, and many of those gifts are precisely suited to the temporal order.

Far fewer people in the parish can supply what the priest was ordained to give. The finance council cannot become a spiritual father in his place. The accountant cannot preach from the priest’s union with Christ. The deacon cannot replace the priest’s sacramental configuration to Christ the Head. The consultant cannot teach prayer from a holiness the priest has not received. Nemo dat quod non habet. No one gives what he does not have.

A priest sent to study nonprofit management before he has been deeply formed in the interior life is being told, perhaps without words, what the institution thinks its crisis is. The same is true when diocesan systems invest more heavily in leadership development than in spiritual fatherhood. The Church may say that holiness is the end, but formation reveals operative trust.

The better order is not administrative ignorance. It is spiritual primacy. Let priests learn enough administration to govern responsibly. Let them rely generously on competent lay experts. Let deacons and finance councils exercise their proper gifts. Then let the priest give himself with greater freedom to the work no one else can do: becoming a holy father of souls.

A parish can survive a priest who needs help reading a balance sheet. It cannot flourish under a priest who does not know the path of prayer.

Why does the Institute speak of bishops as fathers?

The bishop is not merely the executive authority of a territorial unit. He is father of a particular Church.

His sanctifying office is not fulfilled only by ensuring that sacraments are available. He must foster holiness among priests, religious, families, and laity. His priests need more than policies and assignments. They need his paternal presence, his prayer, his knowledge of their burdens, his willingness to walk with them, and his own visible striving for holiness.

A bishop cannot outsource fatherhood. He can delegate administration; he cannot delegate the personal charge to sanctify and shepherd the portion of the People of God entrusted to him.

Leadership

Why does the Institute criticize leadership language in the Church?

Leadership language is often too small for the priesthood and episcopate.

The priest is not essentially a religious executive. The bishop is not essentially a diocesan CEO. Their offices include real governance, but their identity is sacramental and paternal before it is administrative. When the Church borrows leadership models from the corporate world, she risks importing assumptions about effectiveness, control, measurable outcomes, and organizational success that do not correspond to the supernatural order of grace.

The Church needs competent administration; still, she is renewed by saints. She needs fathers more than managers.

Go deeper: Causality

Is the Institute saying the Church should have no leaders?

No. The Church has fathers, shepherds, and governors, and she has always had them. A bishop governs. The Council teaches it, canon law provides for it, and nothing said here disturbs it.

The concern is what governance has been made to look like. Over the last several decades a particular account of governance, drawn from the modern corporation and the business school, has quietly been installed as the form that ecclesial governance takes. The bishop still governs; however, he now governs as a leader governs rather than as a father governs (and his priests know it all too directly), and the difference is not cosmetic. A leader sets a vision, aligns a team, and drives execution toward measurable outcomes. A father begets, feeds, corrects, suffers, and remains. The Church requires the latter, but has become enamored of the first.

Doesn’t Scripture speak of leaders?

It does, and the objection deserves a straight answer. The Letter to the Hebrews tells the faithful, “Obey your leaders and submit to them” (Heb 13:17). The Greek word there, hēgoumenoi, means exactly that. Authority in the Church is real, and it is to be obeyed.

Set beside this the one place where the Gospel comes nearest to the word and does something startling with it. Jesus forbids the title: “Neither be called masters, for you have one master, the Christ” (Mt 23:10). The Greek is kathēgētai, guides or leaders. The disciples are forbidden to be called by the name, because the name belongs to Someone. Beside it stands the scene the Church has never been able to domesticate. The rulers of the Gentiles lord it over their subjects, and Jesus says, “It shall not be so among you” (Mk 10:43). At the Supper Jesus puts it as an image rather than a rule: He is among them as one who serves (cf. Lk 22:27).

Both things are therefore true. There is authority, and it must be obeyed. There is also a name that is reserved and a manner of exercising authority that is forbidden. The tradition has a word for the office that holds both together, and the word is father.

What exactly is the illusion?

The illusion is that fruit follows from competence.

Leadership skills exist and are frequently useful; the deception lies elsewhere. It lies in the quiet conviction, seldom stated and rarely examined, that if the vision were clearer, the team more aligned, the plan more rigorous, and the execution more disciplined, then souls would be converted and the diocese renewed in proportion.

Jesus states the causal fact plainly: “Apart from Me you can do nothing” (Jn 15:5). St. John Paul II applies it directly to pastoral planning and does not soften it. He grants that God asks for real cooperation and for the investment of intelligence and energy, then adds the warning that it is “fatal” to forget that without Christ we can do nothing (NMI, 38). Notice the word. He is not offering a devotional aside at the end of a planning chapter. He is naming a cause of death.

The illusion survives because it is almost never denied. It is affirmed, in the opening prayer of the planning session, and then it is not permitted to govern anything that follows.

What is wrong with servant leadership?

Servant leadership is the most sympathetic form of the problem, and for that reason the hardest to see.

It permits a man to keep the honor while performing its renunciation. He gives a portion of himself, visibly, and receives back the reputation of a man who gives himself. It becomes a transaction.

The self-gift Christ requires does not close, because it is not a transaction. The grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies, and it does not stand at the edge of the furrow to watch what grows (cf. Jn 12:24). What the Church means by service is a life poured out for another with nothing returning to the self, which is why the model is a Cross rather than a seminar. A man may serve genuinely and still find himself sought by the affection of those he has served. The tradition does not condemn him for this. It asks him to notice it, and to give it back.

What does St. Thomas have to do with leadership?

He did the work eight hundred years ago, and the Church has largely forgotten that he did.

In the Summa Contra Gentiles St. Thomas takes up, one by one, the goods in which men place their happiness, and sets each aside. Happiness is not in wealth (SCG III, 33). It is not in honors, nor in fame, nor in power. It is not even in the acts of the moral virtues (SCG III, 34), which is the subtlest of them all, because a man may come to rest in the possession of his own goodness. Happiness is in God, seen and loved, and in nothing else.

Now consider what the leadership imagination offers a man. Recognition of his excellence, which is honor. A reputation extending beyond his sight, which is fame. The capacity to move persons and resources toward ends he has chosen, which is power. The interior satisfaction of possessing his own virtue as an achievement, which is the last one on the list. Leadership does not invent a new idol. It gathers four old ones into a single respectable vocabulary and supplies them with a mission statement.

Every one of these is a genuine good, and that is the entire difficulty. A man pursuing money can be shown a Gospel text. A man pursuing the esteem attached to sacrificial service already has the text, framed, on his wall.

Go deeper: Why Holiness Is Replaced

Isn’t faith the point? Why does the Institute insist on love?

Because St. Paul does. Faith and hope belong to the journey; charity is already the beginning of what will not end. “So faith, hope, love abide, these three; but the greatest of these is love” (1 Cor 13:13).

St. Thomas supplies the reason underneath. Charity is the form of the virtues, the life that orders all the rest toward God, so that faith without charity remains real assent that has not yet become life. St. James makes the same point with a jolt: the demons believe, and shudder (cf. Jas 2:19).

This bears directly on leadership. Faith can be held while a man’s life remains organized around himself. Love cannot. Love is the total gift of self for the sake of the other, and no leadership framework has ever possessed a mechanism for producing it or a way of recognizing it once it appears.

Does this critique indict one side of the Church?

Quite the contrary. The substitution of technique for holiness is the property of no wing. Right worship can be enthroned in the place of God. So can doctrinal correctness, moral rectitude, charitable activity, and social engagement. Each is a real good, and each becomes a substitute at the moment it becomes final.

A reader who arrives hoping to find his ecclesial opponents named will be disappointed, and the disappointment is intended. The disorder described here is found in the most traditional diocese and in the most progressive one, wearing different clothes, speaking a different dialect, doing the same damage.

Metrics and Renewal

Is The Holiness Institute anti-institutional?

No. The Church is visible, sacramental, hierarchical, and institutional. Structures matter.

The Institute’s concern is proportion. Institutions are healthy when they serve holiness. They become spiritually dangerous when they preserve themselves at the expense of holiness, family life, priestly fatherhood, prayer, or sacramental fruitfulness. The question is not whether structures should exist. The question is whether they remain transparent to grace.

Every Catholic institution should be able to answer one question plainly: how does this structure form saints?

What is wrong with using money, attendance, and program participation as metrics?

Nothing is wrong with counting what can be counted. The danger is treating what can be counted as though it were what matters most.

Budgets, attendance, enrollment, and participation can reveal something real. They cannot measure holiness directly. They cannot tell whether a child has learned to pray, whether a marriage has deepened in self-gift, whether a priest is becoming a father, whether a parish is growing in charity, or whether a family is living as a domestic Church.

The Church should count activity honestly. She should judge activity spiritually.

Is stewardship a bad word?

Stewardship is a good and necessary word when it names the grateful return of all things to God.

The word becomes distorted when it is used primarily inside fundraising campaigns. Then a spiritual truth begins to function as a revenue instrument. The faithful are asked to think about conversion, sacrifice, and trust in God, while the operative goal is often institutional funding.

The more searching question is this: if the faithful were truly being fathered, sanctified, and drawn into communion, would generosity need to be engineered? A people gives to what it loves. It loves what gives life.

What would renewal actually look like?

Renewal would look quieter than most Catholics expect.

It would look like bishops praying more and managing less. Priests becoming fathers rather than functionaries. Families reclaiming their mission as domestic churches. Parishes judging programs by whether they form saints. Schools strengthening parents rather than replacing them. Confession becoming a place of conversion rather than occasional maintenance. The Eucharist being received with awe, repentance, and love. The rosary becoming a school of contemplation rather than a badge of identity.

It would look like the Church believing again that holiness is enough because Christ is enough.

Go deeper: Path to Renewal

What question should every bishop, pastor, parent, and Catholic institution ask?

The question is simple enough to remember and demanding enough to reorder everything:

Is this forming saints?

If the answer is unclear, the work needs examination. If the answer is no, the work needs pruning. If the answer is yes, the work should be protected, deepened, and entrusted to God with gratitude.

The Church does not need another way to count activity. She needs to recover the end by which activity is judged.
